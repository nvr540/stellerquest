INTRODUCTION

An exoplanet is a planet that orbits a star outside of our Solar System. These stars are not the Sun, but other stars within or beyond the Milky Way galaxy. Exoplanets are not stars themselves; they are planets similar to Earth, Mars, or Jupiter, but they orbit stars other than the Sun

K2-18 b

K2-18 b, or K-18 b, is an exoplanet about 124 light-years away in the constellation Leo. Discovered in 2015, it is a super-Earth with a radius approximately 2.6 times that of Earth and orbits within the habitable zone of its host star, K2-18. Notably, K2-18 b has shown signs of a hydrogen-rich atmosphere, raising the possibility of water vapor and making it a key target in the search for extraterrestrial life.

Project

Hubble

Hubble's powerful optics have captured stunning cosmic images and directly imaged exoplanets, including Fomalhaut b. Astronomers still analyze its photos for previously undetected exoplanets.

Mission

Kepler

After nine years in deep space, NASA's Kepler telescope, which discovered over 2,600 exoplanets, ran out of fuel in 2018 and was retired in a safe orbit. Its legacy reveals a sky filled with billions of hidden planets, many potential havens for life.

Mission

Spitzer

Spitzer, designed for infrared observation, revolutionized exoplanet study by being the first to directly detect exoplanet light, revealing their composition, temperature, and wind patterns.

Mission

Roman

The Nance Grace Roman Space Telescope (formerly known as WFIRST) is a NASA observatory designed to settle essential questions in the areas of dark energy, exoplanets and infrared astrophysics.

Mission

TESS

The Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS) is searching for the most promising exoplanet targets for next-generation studies.
